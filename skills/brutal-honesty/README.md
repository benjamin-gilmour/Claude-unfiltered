# 🔨 Brutal Honesty

Direct, specific feedback when the work isn't good enough — and genuine recognition when it is.

---

## What it does

When you're fishing for validation on something that has real problems, or when you explicitly ask for straight talk, this skill tells you what's actually wrong. Not vaguely — specifically, with a fix. It leads with the truth, names the exact problem, shows what better looks like, and ends with what's genuinely working. Because there's nearly always something, and it doesn't lead with that.

If the work is actually good, it says so clearly. Empty criticism is as useless as empty praise.

---

## When it fires

**Fires when:**
- You submit something and ask for feedback while clearly hoping for applause
- You explicitly ask for brutal honesty, straight talk, or "tell me what you really think"
- The honest answer differs meaningfully from what you seem to want to hear

**Does not fire when:**
- The work is genuinely solid
- You're asking about personal life choices — no right or wrong answer, no business of this skill
- You're in a fragile emotional state — it reads the room

---

## Quick example

> **You:** Here's my landing page copy — what do you think?
>
> **Claude:** Right. You asked for honest. Here it is.
>
> The headline buries the actual value proposition in the third sentence. By then most people have left. The opening line — "In today's digital landscape..." — is the kind of thing that gets written when nobody knows what to say first. Cut it entirely. Start with what you do.
>
> The CTA says "Learn More." Learn more what? Learn more how? "See how it works" or "Get started free" — something that tells them what happens when they click.
>
> What's working: the three-feature breakdown in the middle is clear and specific. That section is actually doing its job. Build from there.
>
> **[Show me specifically how to fix it]** / **[Give me the full picture]** / **[I hear you — let me rethink]**
